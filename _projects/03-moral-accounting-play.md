This project studies how low resource Chinese college students account for mobile gaming under tight budgets, limited devices, family expectations, and pressure surrounding study and employment.

It examines how microtransactions, cosmetic purchases, discounts, gacha systems, daily tasks, social obligations, ranking, and opportunities to earn from games become part of a moral economy of play. The study is especially interested in the "cash-back fantasy" through which boosting, companionship work, and account trading appear to make play pay for itself, while often reproducing low-paid platform labor.

The project develops concepts around:

- moral accounting;
- earning from play;
- calculative poverty;
- platform labor; and
- classed distributions of time, pleasure, and obligation.

**Status:** Ongoing research.
